Why Measurement Markings Matter for Espresso

Espresso is a precision beverage. The SCA defines a standard double espresso (doppio) as a 1:2 brew ratio — meaning 18–20g of ground coffee produces roughly 36–40ml of liquid in 25–30 seconds. Eyeballing that volume in an unmarked glass makes dialing in your grind and dose a guessing game.

A quality shot glass with dual ml/oz markings lets you verify your yield instantly, without scales or stopwatches. Over hundreds of morning shots, this simple feedback loop genuinely improves consistency.

Materials: What Your Shot Glass Is Made Of Matters

Borosilicate Glass

Borosilicate glass is the gold standard for espresso shot glasses. It handles thermal shock well — important when hot espresso hits a cool glass — and its clarity lets you observe shot color, crema thickness, and flow rate in real time.

Etched markings on borosilicate glass are permanent. Unlike screen-printed or painted-on numbers, etched scales won't fade after repeated dishwasher cycles. Look for dual ml/oz scales on opposite sides for maximum versatility.

Double-Wall Insulated Glass

Double-wall construction keeps your espresso warmer for longer and prevents the outside of the glass from becoming uncomfortably hot. These are especially useful for lungo or Americano preparation. Measurements are typically screen-printed between the walls or etched on the inner wall.

Stainless Steel

Stainless steel espresso measuring cups sacrifice transparency for durability. You can't watch the shot pull, but laser-etched markings are virtually indestructible. These work well in high-volume home settings or if glass breakage is a concern. Many feature a small pour spout, similar to a jigger.

Ceramic

Ceramic demitasse cups with measurement indicators blend aesthetics with function. They retain heat well and look elegant on a coffee bar. However, printed markings on ceramic can fade over time, and opacity means you can't monitor the shot as it pulls.

Buying Checklist: What to Look For

  • Etched vs. printed markings: Etched markings outlast printed ones through repeated dishwashing. Opt for etched whenever possible.
  • Dual ml/oz scale: Recipes may call for either unit. A dual-scale glass eliminates conversion math.
  • Capacity: Standard shot glasses range from 2 oz to 3 oz. If you regularly pull lungos or ristrettos, a 3 oz glass gives you more flexibility.
  • Spout: A small pour spout reduces spillage and crema disruption when transferring shots.
  • Handle: Bare glasses can get warm. A small handle or thumb groove improves comfort without adding bulk.
  • Drip tray clearance: Measure the space under your portafilter before buying. A tall glass won't fit under lower-clearance machines like the DeLonghi Dedica.
  • Stackability: If counter or cabinet space is limited, stackable designs are practical.

FAQ: Common Questions About Espresso Shot Glasses

What size shot glass should I use for espresso?

A standard single espresso is approximately 30ml (1 oz) and a double is approximately 60ml (2 oz), per SCA guidelines. A 2 oz (60ml) glass covers both volumes. If you pull lungos regularly, opt for a 3 oz (90ml) glass.

Are etched measurements better than printed ones?

For longevity, yes. Etched markings are physically cut into the glass and won't fade or wash off. Printed or screen-printed markings may degrade after many dishwasher cycles, though high-quality ceramic-fired prints can be quite durable.

Which espresso shot glass works best with a Breville or DeLonghi machine?

Both brands have relatively standard drip tray clearances, but the DeLonghi Dedica series has notably limited clearance (around 75mm). Look for shorter, wider shot glasses — roughly 55–60mm tall — to ensure they fit comfortably under the portafilter spout without requiring removal of the drip tray.

Do I need a spouted shot glass?

Not essential, but useful. A spout helps when pouring extracted espresso into a milk pitcher or cup, reducing crema disturbance and spillage. It's a small ergonomic upgrade worth considering if you make lattes or cappuccinos regularly.

Can I use a regular shot glass for espresso measurements?

Standard bar shot glasses (1.5 oz / 44ml) rarely include ml markings and aren't designed for the heat of espresso. A purpose-made borosilicate espresso glass is a better investment for both accuracy and heat resistance.
